THE CHIP DERATING

Three blows at once: overcapacity, custom silicon, and a Chinese open model pushed semis toward a bear market

The AI-chip trade is being attacked from three sides. First, Meta admitted it has excess AI compute and plans to resell it, breaking the assumption that every GPU gets used. Second, a wave of custom in-house silicon began shipping from the hyperscalers, making Nvidia's near-monopoly look contested for the first time in years. Third, and new this week, China's Moonshot AI released Kimi K3, a 2.8-trillion-parameter open-source model that benchmarks neck-and-neck with the best systems from OpenAI and Anthropic, raising the question of whether ever-more US chips are the only path to frontier AI. The result: global semiconductors have shed about $3.3T since June and are nearing bear-market territory, with the Philadelphia Semiconductor Index off roughly 10%. Friday alone saw AMD -5%, Intel -4%, Nvidia -3% (before recovering). Under the surface the market discriminated: Nvidia held best (down only about 2% on the month, still mid-range, PEG 0.46, 114% ROE), while memory and foundry took the brunt (Micron -17%, TSMC -6% with a heavy-liquidation event, ASML weak).

Why it matters For a long-term buyer, the chip layer is where the AI dollars land first, and this month shows it is also where overcapacity, competition and cost-deflation fears bite first and hardest. The leaders are still not expensive on growth-adjusted valuation, but the safer way to own the layer through this uncertainty is the diversified chip ETF SMH (this month's pick #3, now on a deeper pullback), which spreads you across NVDA, AVGO, TSM and the rest. Keep any single speculative chip name small next to the ETF, own the demand side too (the hyperscalers via VOO), and let hyperscaler order data confirm the thesis before pressing.

Primer: how AI infrastructure makes money, layer by layerexpand

Primer: How AI Infrastructure Makes Money

A plain-English tour of what actually goes into an AI data center and where the dollars land, layer by layer. No jargon required.

A large AI data center is a machine built in physical layers, and money flows into it from the top (the customers who order it) down through every supplier who makes a piece. Here is where the dollars land, and why the least glamorous layers can be the safest way to invest.

Start at the top: the customers

The hyperscalers (Microsoft, Alphabet, Amazon, Meta, Oracle) announce the giant spending budgets, roughly $725B in 2026, that everyone else competes to capture. Owning them is the most diversified way to own the theme, because their core businesses (ads, cloud, software) fund the AI bet and keep earning even if AI spending pauses. Two cautions surfaced this month: Oracle funded its buildout with debt and negative cash flow and got punished (down about 33%), and Meta revealed it has excess compute to resell, a reminder that even well-funded buyers can build ahead of demand.

Layer 1: Silicon, the brains

Nvidia (NVDA) designs the GPUs that do the AI math; AMD (AMD) is the challenger; Broadcom (AVGO) builds the custom chips hyperscalers increasingly design in-house, a trend now challenging Nvidia's near-monopoly. None own factories: TSMC (TSM) prints the chips using machines only ASML (ASML) makes, and every GPU needs memory from Micron (MU). Highest growth, highest risk, and the layer that falls first on any overcapacity or competition scare, as this month proved when a cheaper Chinese open model and custom silicon hit the trade.

Layer 2: The box and the room

The chips get assembled into servers by Super Micro (SMCI) and Dell (DELL). A rack of AI chips runs blazing hot, so it needs advanced cooling and power distribution from Vertiv (VRT), and fast switches from Arista (ANET) to connect racks together. This is the guts of the room.

Layer 3: Moving the data

AI splits one job across thousands of chips, so they must talk at the speed of light over fiber. Transceivers and lasers come from Coherent (COHR) and Lumentum (LITE), long-haul interconnect from Ciena (CIEN), and the actual glass fiber from Corning (GLW). Optics content rises with every hardware generation, though it sells off hard with the chips when the whole AI-hardware trade derates, as it did this month.

Layer 4: The electrical bones

The least glamorous and often most attractive layer: switchgear from Eaton (ETN) and Hubbell (HUBB), the steel and PVC conduit that carries every wire from Atkore (ATKR), jobsite supply from WESCO (WCC), transmission and substation build from Quanta (PWR), and the raw steel from Nucor (NUE). Physical, hard-to-substitute, multi-year backlogs.

Layer 5: The power

A data center is useless without electricity, and AI has made power the true bottleneck. Existing-reactor operators signing long deals are Constellation (CEG) and Vistra (VST); fuel comes from Cameco (CCJ); reactor and SMR technology from BWX (BWXT), Oklo (OKLO) and NuScale (SMR); and near-term gas-turbine and grid power from GE Vernova (GEV). The operators held up far better than the developers in this month's selloff.

Chips versus picks-and-shovels

Follow a dollar of capex: it lands most heavily on the chip layer, which captures the most dollars but carries the most risk (a rival can win a socket, custom silicon can erode the moat, and an overcapacity or competition scare hits it first, as this month proved three times over). The power, conduit, cooling and fiber layers are lower-binary: whatever chip and model win, the building still needs steel, copper, transformers and megawatts. That is the trade-off, highest upside in silicon, more durable margins of safety in the infrastructure beneath it.

Field Guide: The Numbers That Decide a Long-Term Buy

Every metric on the board, what high and low normally mean, and the threshold each Bull Score check uses. These are also the hover tooltips on every column.

P/E & Fwd P/E The price of a dollar of profit

P/E is the share price divided by yearly profit per share: literally the dollars you pay for each dollar the company earns. A LOW P/E (under ~15) normally means the stock is cheap OR the market doubts the profits will last (value trap risk). A HIGH P/E (over ~30) normally means the market expects strong growth and you are pre-paying for it; if the growth disappoints, the multiple deflates hard (this month's AI selloff was exactly that). Forward P/E uses next year's expected profit: when it is far below the trailing P/E, analysts expect earnings to grow into the price. Always compare within an industry, and pair with PEG before judging.

check: Fwd P/E below trailing P/E = earnings expected to grow

PEG The growth-adjusted price tag

P/E divided by the expected profit growth rate. It answers the question P/E alone cannot: is the growth worth the price? Around 1.0 is fair; under 1.0 you are getting growth at a discount; over 2.0 you are paying up on faith.

check: PEG ≤ 1.5 · green ≤ 1.0 · amber > 2.0

ROE The quality engine

Profit per dollar of shareholder capital. This is the compounding engine of a long-term holding: 10-20% is strong, sustained above 20% is elite, negative means the company burns money. High ROE with low debt is the quality signature.

check: ROE ≥ 10% · red if negative

D/E The survival test

Debt versus the shareholders' own money. Low debt means the company controls its fate through downturns and high-rate periods; high debt means refinancing risk decides for it. Matters double while the Fed is hawkish.

check: D/E ≤ 1.5 · green ≤ 0.5

vs 200D The long-term trend

Distance from the 200-day moving average, the classic dividing line between long-term uptrend and downtrend. Above it, time is on your side; below it, you are fighting the tide (fine for slow accumulation of quality, bad for lump sums). More than 10% below is serious trend damage.

check: price above the 200-day · red < -10%

D-Days Is big money leaving?

Distribution days: sessions in the last 25 that fell at least 0.2% on above-average volume, the footprint of institutions selling into the market. A cluster warns you even when price looks fine. ⚠ flags a heavy dump (down ≥1.5% on ≥1.5x volume) within the last 5 sessions.

check: fewer than 5 and no ⚠ · amber at 3-4

Bull Score The verdict column

How many of the six checks above pass right now. 5-6: the data supports a bull case (valuation, quality, safety, trend and flows all agree). 3-4: mixed, the thesis needs a specific reason. 0-2: the data argues against it; if you still want it, you are making a contrarian bet and should size it that way. Hover any score to see exactly which checks fail.

5-6 green · 3-4 amber · 0-2 red

COT Who is positioned where

A timing aid only: it never makes something a good or bad business. The CFTC's weekly Commitments of Traders filing shows the actual futures positions of asset managers (slow, long-term money) and leveraged funds (hedge funds). Watch the week-over-week change, not the level: hedge funds are structurally short index futures as a hedge. Extremes are contrarian: record shorts often precede rallies, record longs precede tops.

published Fridays, data as of Tuesday

Yield curve & VIX The two macro tells
